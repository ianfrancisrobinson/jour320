In late July of 2012, Angie Epifano sat on a field behind Amherst College and wrote in her journal for about three hours. She had realized that what happened to her more than a year before as a college freshman was something that she “should not and could not forget and could never forgive.”

Epifano was raped by an acquaintance in her college dormitory on May 25, 2011. That July journal entry about her sexual assault experience and Amherst’s poor response eventually became a published letter in the school’s independent newspaper, The Amherst Student. Epifano’s letter was read by so many people that it crashed the website’s servers and sparked a movement for other sexual assault survivors to speak out.

“Survivors are coming out, they’re telling their stories, and people are realizing these people aren’t just one student in a school of 20,000 that I’ll never meet,” Epifano said. “This is the student next to me, this is my best friend, this is my girlfriend or boyfriend. And that personal connection is what’s giving it power, and is what’s making people realize this is a human rights issue.”
